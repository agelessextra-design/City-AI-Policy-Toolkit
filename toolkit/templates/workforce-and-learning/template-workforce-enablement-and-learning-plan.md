# [[CITY_NAME]] Workforce Enablement And Learning Plan For AI

## Purpose

This plan defines how [[CITY_NAME]] will prepare staff, managers, HR partners, and labor stakeholders for responsible AI use.

It turns workforce readiness into a managed operating requirement instead of an afterthought to technology rollout.

## Scope

This plan applies to:

- staff who use approved AI tools
- managers and supervisors overseeing affected workflows
- HR, training, labor, and employee-relations partners
- teams piloting or scaling AI-enabled service changes

This plan does not replace:

- governance approval requirements
- job classification or bargaining obligations
- service-specific implementation playbooks
- public-facing transparency or notice materials

## Workforce Principles

The city will manage AI adoption in ways that:

- keep human accountability clear
- support staff capability before expecting changed performance
- surface labor and employee-relations impacts early
- avoid hidden role drift
- distinguish experimentation from production expectations
- align training with actual tools, workflows, and risk levels

Possible learning tracks:

- awareness and acceptable-use basics
- prompt and tool-use practice for approved low-risk tasks
- manager oversight and review expectations
- specialist training for builders, analysts, or product owners
- executive decision and risk oversight training

Managers should not be expected to oversee AI use without explicit support.

The city should define:

- how managers approve or monitor day-to-day use
- how they identify training completion
- how they escalate misuse, drift, or workflow breakdown
- how they support staff adoption without forcing unapproved workarounds

## Support Model

The city should define where staff go for help.

Support channels may include:

- approved knowledge base or intranet guidance
- office hours or drop-in support
- manager support guides
- help desk or digital services intake
- designated AI champions or practice leads

The city must decide:

- whether support is centralized, distributed, or hybrid
- who owns training updates
- who owns tool-specific guidance

[[REQUIRES_LOCAL_DECISION: support ownership model]]

## Adoption Guardrails

Training and enablement should reinforce the city’s operating rules.

The plan must state that staff may not:

- use unapproved tools for sensitive work
- upload restricted data to unapproved systems
- treat AI outputs as final without required review
- bypass governance, records, accessibility, or service standards

The city should also define how approved experimentation is distinguished from prohibited behavior.

## Measurement And Feedback

The city should track whether workforce adoption is healthy and supportable.

Measures may include:

- training completion by role
- support request volume and recurring issues
- adoption by approved use case
- manager confidence and staff feedback
- incidents, near misses, or misuse trends

## Review And Refresh

This plan should be reviewed when:

- new AI tools or higher-risk uses are introduced
- workforce impacts change materially
- labor or HR obligations change
- staff support demand outgrows the current model
- the city moves from pilot to broader deployment

- who owns plan maintenance
- how revisions are approved
- when a workforce plan update is required before scale-up
