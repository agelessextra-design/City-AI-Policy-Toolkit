# [[CITY_NAME]] Public Trust And Engagement Framework For AI

## Purpose

This framework defines how [[CITY_NAME]] will explain, discuss, and stay accountable for AI use with residents, community partners, and the public.

It is the community-trust spine for the toolkit and should be used alongside governance, service delivery, and workforce materials.

## Scope

This framework applies to:

- resident-facing AI-enabled services
- citywide public explanation of AI use
- channels for questions, concerns, and feedback
- accessibility, language-access, and trust commitments related to AI use

This framework does not replace:

- legal disclosure obligations
- formal appeals processes required by local law
- operational incident response procedures
- service-specific notices where additional detail is needed

## Public Trust Principles

The city should manage AI use in ways that:

- explain meaningful use in plain language
- make human accountability visible
- provide residents with a path to ask questions or raise concerns
- avoid hidden harm or confusion for people affected by AI-enabled services
- treat accessibility and language access as core requirements
- update public information when material changes occur

## Public Explanation Model

The city should define what it will explain publicly and where.

Possible explanation channels:

- central AI information page
- service-specific notices
- public register or accountability list
- community briefings or listening sessions
- annual or periodic updates

The city must decide:

- which AI uses require public explanation
- who approves public-language descriptions
- which channels are mandatory

## Notice And Disclosure Triggers

The city should identify when residents must receive direct or prominent notice.

Examples:

- an AI-enabled tool is used in a resident-facing workflow
- a resident submits information to an AI-enabled process
- a city decision-support workflow materially shapes a resident interaction
- a material change in service design changes how AI is used

The city must decide:

- which notices are standard
- which notices are service-specific
- which uses require only register-level transparency

## Community Engagement Approach

The city should define how it will hear from the public before and after deploying meaningful AI uses.

Possible engagement methods:

- targeted listening sessions
- community advisory review
- resident surveys
- service-specific feedback loops
- feedback through existing neighborhood or board structures

The city must decide:

- when engagement is required
- which communities need targeted outreach
- how feedback changes decisions

## Incident Communication

When AI use contributes to a meaningful service, trust, or safety incident, the city should define:

- who decides what is communicated publicly
- when communication is required
- how impacted residents are informed
- how lessons learned are shared internally and, when appropriate, externally

[[REQUIRES_LOCAL_DECISION: public incident communication threshold]]

If the use is publicly controversial, politically sensitive, or likely to receive external scrutiny, the framework should also state:

- what the city is and is not doing
- who owns inquiry, complaint, correction, and redress routing
- what remains blocked or unresolved
- what public reporting point comes next
